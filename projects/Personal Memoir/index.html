Two Miles of Trouble

Sunlight filtered through the trees on the trail, and the air smelled fresh and damp. There I was, about 30 feet away from the Potomac river, hiking down the trail that leads through the river. It was about 9 in the morning, and I was feeling great. I was getting sunlight, getting physical activity, and having the time of my life. I was just eight years old, marching down the Potomac trail, alone.

We had arrived early in the morning, about eight a.m., to avoid the midday crowd. The air was still cool and the birds were singing loudly. My parents and siblings were refreshed, ready to get some hiking in before lunchtime. I was buzzing with the energy that only an eight year old could possibly have. The trail we were hiking wasn’t particularly difficult, other than the occasional mud and the fallen tree near the start, it was about as easy as it gets. The trail led us through the tall oak trees in the direction of the Potomac, alive and murky as it was in 2018.

Once we got there, we found our trail and started hiking. We hiked for about half an hour, walking through piles of mud and natural bridges. After about half an hour, my parents got tired and slowed down. My siblings seemed to catch on, and slowed down as well. When we reached a comfortable stopping point, my parents stopped and called out, “We’re taking a break here!”

My siblings heard loud and clear, but for me, it went in one ear and out the other. I didn’t hear them, or probably didn’t want to. I was very energetic at that age, and maybe I didn’t want to stop. I kept walking, pushing on the dirt like it had offended me. I assumed my family was right behind me. Why wouldn’t they be? They couldn’t have possibly felt tired if I didn’t feel tired. That’s now how it worked.

I continued to walk through the trail, completely unaware that I had abandoned my family back at the stopping place. My parents, noticing their eight year old child had just abandoned them, stopped their break and started to follow me, likely calling out my name. If they did, I never heard them call out my name, and I kept walking. As I walked, I noticed that many people on the trail were looking at me, confused and puzzled. Little did I know it was because a child had endangered himself by abandoning his parents with no way to communicate back to them .

I could almost hear them thinking, What is this kid doing right now? I tried to act like any other hiker, giving them a shy, awkward “Hi” as they walked by. Inside, I felt a little embarrassed, and could tell something wasn’t right. I thought, “Why would adults be looking at me like this? They didn’t do that the other times I went hiking.” Even with the looks, I continued to walk through the trails.

The forest grew quieter, the trail stretched on. My legs started to ache, but I pushed forward, oddly stubborn and arrogant. I was convinced that my parents were also getting tired, so I looked back. I was a little surprised to see no one behind me. But I thought to myself, “Maybe I was just way ahead of them. I’m usually ahead when we’re hiking, so maybe I was a little far ahead.”

After about an hour, I finally stopped. I was breathing heavily, my legs were burning, and the excitement that I had felt an hour ago faded. I decided to stay here until my parents had caught up, and that’s when I heard it. “Patrick!” The voice echoed through the forest. “Patrick!” It grew louder, closer, angrier.

I decided to walk towards the sound, and then I saw them. My parents, red-faced, sweaty, embarrassed, and furious. My siblings stood next to them, tired and excited that they could see their brother again. When I reached up to them, I learned that I had walked almost 2 miles by myself, and that we would have to walk back.

During the return hike, my parents weren’t happy. They lectured me about listening, safety, and how some places aren’t playgrounds. I didn’t argue, I couldn’t argue. They weren’t wrong. I didn’t have a strong rebuttal, or a good reason why I walked two miles.

That day stuck with me. It taught me that independence isn’t ignorance, that you should listen to those around you and who care about you. It taught me that listening is important. It taught me to pause for a second and look around. Most importantly, it taught me to walk with my family, and not in front of them.
